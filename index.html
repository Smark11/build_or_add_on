The financial crux

The "golden handcuffs": a sub-4% mortgage is an asset worth protecting.

If the family holds a pandemic-era mortgage — statistically likely, since 52.5% of mortgaged U.S. owners do — then moving re-prices their entire balance from ~3% to today's 6.48%. Adding on borrows only the new piece. This is why building stays competitive with moving instead of being far worse.

The "rate penalty" of giving up the low rate — about $2,400/yr for every $100,000 borrowed, before any price step-up. On a $600K loan that's over $1,200/month of pure penalty.

An addition keeps the cheap mortgage intact

A renovation funded by a fixed second lien (home-equity loan) leaves the first mortgage untouched and only borrows the project cost at today's rates. A cash-out refinance or full reno loan, by contrast, re-prices the whole balance — usually the worst move for a sub-4% holder.

The 1–5 year forecast — why waiting is a trap

What's likely to come on the market, and when.

Simsbury turns over only ~2.5–3% of homes a year (≈30-year tenure). A "silver tsunami" — 29% of the town is 55+ — adds supply, but slowly. Connecticut is lagging the national inventory thaw, so relief comes late and weak here.

"Waiting most likely means paying more for less choice — while staying cramped." — Forecast synthesis · all four experts concur

New construction won't help: the big pipeline item (the former Hartford Insurance campus, ~689 units) is overwhelmingly contested apartments, not the 4-bedroom single-family homes this family needs.

    The recommendation

    Stay and build a modest addition — one that protects a once-in-a-generation mortgage rate and the school zone you already have.

    A focused ~$100–150K living-space addition — finish the basement, then a rear great room (no new bedroom, so no septic review) — financed by a fixed second lien, is the rate-efficient, school-secure choice that stays under the street's value ceiling, provided a short checklist clears. Option B (buy a turnkey in-zone home like 5 Lawton Dr) is a legitimate co-finalist if disruption is unacceptable, the addition proves infeasible, or the current rate turns out not to be low. Option C — waiting — is not recommended.
